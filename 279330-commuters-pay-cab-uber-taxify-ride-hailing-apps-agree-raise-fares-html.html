Commuters to pay more for cab as Uber, Taxify and other ride-hailing apps agree to raise fares

- The taxi-hailing apps agreed to increase the fares after reaching an agreement with the Ministry of Transport

- The concerned parties also engage private security companies for quick response to emergencies

- This was after concerns were raised by the service providers of drivers having lost their lives while on duty

- The drivers took to the streets on July 2, 2018 to protest unfair rates

Just days after a section of drivers with taxi-hailing companies took to the streets to protest the rates, they can now breathe a sigh of relief after the companies agreed to raise fares following talks between the drivers and Ministry of Transport.

Parties involved signed a legally non-binding MoU to cushion drivers from price differentials during price changes.

'Price considerations per km by Digital App Taxi Providers will be guided by the average operating vehicle cost and classification of vehicles as provided by the Automobile Association guidelines," read part of the agreement.

The agreement also said the concerned parties will engage private security companies for quick response in case of criminal, emergency and breakdown incidents.

These services would be provided to drivers while they are online which comes as a major concession after the drivers' union claimed almost 35 drivers have died while on duty since November, 2017.

"Digital Taxi Forum will sensitise their drivers and driver-partners against engaging in violent acts that are criminal in nature that threaten and intimidate industry players and riders," the agreement read.'"All vehicles operating as digital app taxis must be inspected by NTSA in line with the relevant standards," it stated.

The agreemnent also went as far as declaring that cab-hailing apps were transport companies not tech providers.

The agreement followed a strike by drivers from Uber, Little Cab, Taxify and Mondo who were against the low rates charged by service providers which squeezed their commissions.

But money has not been the only challenge for the employees as many have faced hostility from other taxi drivers.

In March 2016, six men were charged with malicious damage to property and attempted murder following an attack on an Uber driver.
