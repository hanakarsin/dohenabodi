Opinion: Kenya is a beautiful country suffering from deep-seated trust crisis

One of the biggest challenges facing Kenya right now is a deep trust crisis among the citizenry; at both a personal and societal level.

PAY ATTENTION: Don't miss trending Kenyan news. Follow TUKO.co.ke on Twitter!

Our level of trust, whether in the government, public institutions, elected officials, the media or even in each other, is frighteningly declining with every sunset. We no longer readily trust anyone or anything anymore.

While we all depend on a shared currency of trust to drive our social, business, financial, political and other affairs, we have critically devalued this currency and taken breaches of trust to shocking proportions.

The crisis was laid bare for all to see as the government kicked-off the countrywide rollout of the AstraZeneca COVID-19 vaccines last week.

Many Kenyans were sceptical about the entire issue. Some were worried about the vaccine’s safety; others questioned whether what we saw Acting Director of Health Patrick Amoth being injected with was actually the vaccine or plain water.

There were also those who wanted to know why our political leaders were not in the frontline to set the pace and create some confidence among the masses that the jab was safe and so on and so forth.

All these fears, hesitation, and speculations boil down to trust issues.

We have been lied to so many times by the people we trust most that we no longer take what others say without a doubt.

The mistrust runs deep across all spheres of life. Our professionals no longer base their arguments and actions on facts and stats but are driven by greed, tribalism and political inclinations. They will shamelessly distort facts to suit their selfish agenda regardless of the repercussions of their action.

Our religious leaders are no longer guided by “roho mtakatifu” (Holy Spirit) but by “roho mtakakitu”. Without blinking an eye, they will mislead their flock and skew their arguments where their stomachs are.

This is the reason it is so difficult for Kenyans to tell what is good for them. They have no one to trust with their lives or wellbeing.

Distrust does not happen overnight. It develops progressively through stages, especially after one discovers that the other party lied to them on a certain matter or issue. The experiences we go through with other people and especially those entrusted with leadership positions in any field of life impact our ability to trust others.

Past disappointments or betrayals are at the root of this issue because mistrust is a valid response to feeling betrayed.

Financial and economic challenges also lead to a significant loss of trust in the government. There is flagrant dishonesty among those we have entrusted with leadership positions. Many of them would rather take shortcuts or make populist interventions that eventually lead to even more problems and distrust.

This is a great predicament as low trust in those in leadership positions makes it harder to solve many of the country’s problems.

A decline in trust leads to lower rates of compliance with rules and regulations. It makes citizens become more risk-averse, thus delaying investment, innovation and employment decisions that are essential to regain competitiveness and spur growth.

Is there a way to address the erosion of trust? Definitely yes.

The best way to prevent distrust from taking root is by proactively focusing on building trust. Trust must be continually developed and nurtured; not just when it is at risk.

Trust in government by citizens is essential for effective and efficient policymaking. Those in leadership should invest in trust in order to reinforce social cohesion and restore economic growth.

Professionals must only be guided by the desire for service delivery and integrity. Rather than being merchants of misery, the clergy should sincerely take up their cardinal role of being the “good shepherds” and inspire hope in their flock. It is a journey we must walk together to make our country great again.
