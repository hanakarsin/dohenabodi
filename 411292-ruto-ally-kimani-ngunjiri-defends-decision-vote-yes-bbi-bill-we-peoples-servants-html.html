Ruto Ally Kimani Ngunjiri Defends Decision to Vote Yes for BBI Bill: &quot;We're People's Servants&quot;

- Bahati MP Kimani Ngunjiri was among seven allies of Deputy President William Ruto who voted in favour of the BBI bill

- Ruto's Tanga Tanga members have in the past urged their supporters to reject the bill, which they claimed would only benefit a few people

- The Bahati lawmaker said the fact that he voted against the wish of Ruto's supporters should not be seen as a betrayal

Bahati MP Kimani Ngunjiri has come under fierce criticisms and online ridicule after he voted in support of the Building Bridges Initiative (BBI) bill against his Tanga Tanga members' wish.

Ngunjiri, David ole Sankok (nominated), Kareke Mbiuki (Kaara), Joash Nyamoko (South Mugirango), and Charity Kathambi (Njoro), who are key figures in Deputy President William Ruto's camp, supported the bill.

Perhaps seen as one of the vocal Ruto loyalists and the most visible one in Nakuru county, the decision by the Bahati legislator did not go unnoticed and expectedly attracted some debate.

Change of heart

However, Ngunjiri defended his democratic right explaining his surprise change of heart on the bill he previously campaigned against should not mean betraying Ruto.

"My vote does not in any way amount to a betrayal of William Ruto. I voted yes because of the benefits we are getting as Nakuru county.......As for the MP's that voted NO, I respect their decision. We are our people's servants and we have to represent their views," he stated.

If passed, the Constitution of Kenya Amendment Bill 2020 will see Nakuru county get five additional constituencies, which means more money will be allocated for the residents.

"Getting five more constituencies and more funds to the county government means we will have more development at the grassroots level. My conscience, therefore, could not allow me to vote against more money to the grassroots," the MP argued.

During the heated session that came to a close past midnight on Thursday, May 7, the bill went through the second and third reading without introducing any amendments.

Second reading

A total of 247 legislators were physically present inside the National Assembly, while another 55 who had not turned up registered for the virtual voting.

Constitutionally, only 175 MPs were needed for the bill to proceed to the next stage, the Committee of the Whole Stage.

The Speaker announced 235 against 83 members had voted in support of the bill while two lawmakers opted to abstain from the exercise allowing the bill to move to the third reading.

Third reading

At this point, it was the turn of Majority Leader Amos Kimunya to move the motion afresh and pave way for the third reading, but without a single amendment as always been the case.

During this stage, a total of 224 MPs voted for the bill against 63 who dissented. Two members abstained.
